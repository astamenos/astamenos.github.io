<section id="statistics" class="fade-in">
  <p class="section-label">Field Note 01 · Statistics</p>
  <h2 class="about-heading">Quantifying what we do not know</h2>
  <div class="bio-body">
    <p>Statistics is the discipline of reasoning under uncertainty, done honestly. I work in the Bayesian tradition, where probability is a measure of belief rather than long-run frequency. You start with what you know, observe data, and update. Prior plus evidence gives you a posterior. The framework forces you to state your assumptions out loud, which is the part I trust most.</p>
    <p>My research sits at the harder end of this: Bayesian spatiotemporal models for neuroimaging, causal inference, and high-dimensional problems where the honest answer is a distribution, not a point. I care about effect sizes and credible intervals, not asterisks next to p-values. A number without its uncertainty is a claim without a spine.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="psychopathology" class="fade-in">
  <p class="section-label">Field Note 02 · Psychopathology</p>
  <h2 class="about-heading">How harm patterns in people</h2>
  <div class="bio-body">
    <p>The old model sorted mental illness into discrete boxes. The newer one, HiTOP, treats it as a set of dimensions: spectra of antagonism, disinhibition, detachment, and internalizing that shade continuously from ordinary into pathological. This is a better map. Real people do not fit in boxes, and pretending they do costs the people who are hardest to categorize.</p>
    <p>I am most interested in antagonism, the externalizing pattern where charm, deceit, and a talent for exploiting others cohere into something predictable. When you apply normal-person logic to someone operating on antagonistic logic, you mispredict them every time. Learning to close that gap is not academic for me. It is protective.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="cybernetics" class="fade-in">
  <p class="section-label">Field Note 03 · Cybernetics</p>
  <h2 class="about-heading">Systems that regulate themselves, or fail to</h2>
  <div class="bio-body">
    <p>Cybernetics is the science of feedback, control, and communication in systems, whether biological, mechanical, social, or computational. Norbert Wiener founded it. The central question is simple to state and endless to answer: how does a system steer itself toward a goal?</p>
    <p>The loops that fascinate me are the ones that run away. Positive feedback amplifies. A small deviation grows instead of settling, and the system locks into a dysfunctional state that sustains itself. You see the same shape in Ponzi schemes, in cycles of abuse, in intergenerational trauma, and in Colin DeYoung's cybernetic model of personality, where psychopathology is feedback failure. One structure, many disguises. Learning to see it is learning to name what was happening while it happened.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="mathematics" class="fade-in">
  <p class="section-label">Field Note 04 · Mathematics</p>
  <h2 class="about-heading">The structure underneath the methods</h2>
  <div class="bio-body">
    <p>Every method I use rests on mathematics, and I would rather understand the foundation than borrow the result. Probability, linear algebra, and the analysis that makes an estimator behave are not decoration on top of statistics. They are the reason it works at all.</p>
    <p>What draws me is proof as a form of honesty. A proof cannot flatter you, cannot round in your favor, cannot lean on authority. It either holds or it does not. In a world that runs on persuasion, that kind of certainty is rare, and worth the effort it demands.</p>
  </div>
</section>

<section id="technology" class="fade-in">
  <p class="section-label">Field Note 05 · Technology</p>
  <h2 class="about-heading">Tools that hold up under real stakes</h2>
  <div class="bio-body">
    <p>I spent years building federal-grade systems: high-throughput data pipelines, machine learning and NLP tooling in compliance-constrained environments, systems that could not fail during a national public health response. That work taught me what most demos never test. A tool is only as good as its behavior on the worst day, in the hands of the person who needs it most.</p>
    <p>So I keep returning to human-centered design, to cybersecurity, and to AI safety as an alignment problem, which is really a control problem in cybernetic clothing. The question is always the same. Does this system serve the human in front of it, or does it serve itself? I build for the first answer.</p>
  </div>
</section>
