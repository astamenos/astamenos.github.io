# ── About ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
about:
  heading: What the book is
  body: >
    OUROBOROS is narrative nonfiction that reads a lived experience of coercive control through two
    rigorous frameworks: the cybernetics of feedback and the dimensional model of psychopathology.
    It is a memoir and a scholarly treatment at once, written with the controlled precision of someone
    who documented everything because documentation was the only tool available. It deals in mental illness,
    abuse, and the failures of the institutions that promise protection. It is dark and vengeful in places,
    scientifically careful throughout, and, underneath both, full of hope.

# ── Pull quote ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
pull_quote: >
  "The ouroboros does not choose to devour itself. It is built to. This book is an argument that the repetition is not inevitable, and that naming it with precision is the first step out."

    heading: Antagonism
    body_paragraphs:
      - >
        The people at the center of this story share a recognizable pattern, not a diagnosis but a profile.
        They present as exceptionally charming, capable, and often accomplished. They construct relationships
        through idealization and discard. They lie not occasionally but constitutively: the lie is the primary
        medium through which they interact with the world.
      - >
        The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology, or HiTOP, replaces the old categorical boxes with a single
        externalizing spectrum, situating narcissistic, borderline, and antisocial patterns along measurable
        dimensions of antagonism and disinhibition. When you apply normal-person logic to someone operating on
        antagonistic logic, you mispredict their behavior every time. This book works to close that gap.

  - id: trauma
    heading: Trauma
    body_paragraphs:
      - >
        Trauma produced by sustained antagonism differs in character from acute trauma.
        It compounds slowly, invisibly, and in ways that institutions are poorly designed to detect.
        The person being harmed often cannot name what is happening to them, because the harm is
        designed to be unnameable, to feel like ordinary life, to locate the problem in the victim's
        perception rather than the perpetrator's conduct.
      - >
        Complex trauma of this kind reshapes the nervous system, alters the capacity for trust,
        and systematically dismantles the structures, relationships, career, finances, self-concept,
        that might otherwise provide resilience. Recovery is not a matter of processing a single event.
        It is the long work of rebuilding what was taken apart.

  - id: betrayal
    heading: Institutional Betrayal
    body_paragraphs:
      - >
        Betrayal trauma theory, developed by Jennifer Freyd, describes the particular damage done when the
        systems a person depends on for protection become the source of further harm. The literature on this,
        including the research on secondary victimization by protective systems, lives in academic journals that
        survivors cannot read and courts do not cite.
      - >
        OUROBOROS treats institutional failure as a systems problem, not a personal grievance. A system that
        cannot register corrective feedback consumes itself, the same runaway loop that antagonistic personality
        pathology enacts at the scale of a person. Naming that structure, and building the mechanisms that
        interrupt it, is the work this book argues for.
