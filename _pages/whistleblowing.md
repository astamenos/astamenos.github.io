<p class="section-label">Advocacy Note 01 · The problem</p>

## Institutional betrayal

When a person depends on an institution for protection, and that institution responds to a report of harm by protecting itself instead, the injury compounds. The psychologist Jennifer Freyd named this **institutional betrayal**: the failure of an organization to prevent or respond supportively to wrongdoing committed within it, especially wrongdoing it had a duty to address.

Betrayal by a trusted system is not the same as an isolated bad outcome. The research on **betrayal trauma** shows that harm delivered by the very source a person relies on produces distinct and lasting damage, precisely because the person cannot easily withdraw their trust without losing the support they need.

<p class="section-label">Advocacy Note 02 · The tactic</p>

## DARVO

When someone reports misconduct, a predictable pattern often follows. Freyd calls it **DARVO**: Deny, Attack, and Reverse Victim and Offender. The person or institution responsible denies the conduct, attacks the credibility of the person reporting it, and recasts themselves as the true victim of an unfair accusation.

Naming DARVO matters because it is designed to disorient. Research finds that simply understanding the tactic reduces its power to make an observer, or a target, doubt what happened. If you have reported harm and found the response aimed at you rather than the conduct, you were not imagining the reversal. It has a name and a literature.

<p class="section-label">Advocacy Note 03 · The antidote</p>

## Institutional courage

The opposite of institutional betrayal is not neutrality. It is **institutional courage**: the commitment of an organization to seek the truth and act on it, even at a cost to itself. In practice that means responding to reports with transparency instead of defensiveness, protecting the people who come forward, and treating accountability as a form of integrity rather than a threat to reputation.

Courage of this kind is a design choice. Systems that build real mechanisms for feedback and correction can interrupt the cycle. Systems that cannot register correction tend to repeat the harm.

<p class="section-label">Advocacy Note 04 · You are not alone</p>

## If you are reporting harm

Reporting wrongdoing and surviving the retaliation that can follow is isolating work. It helps to know that peer support exists, that the psychology of retaliation is documented, and that the frameworks above were built by researchers precisely so that people in your position would have language for what is happening.

A few public resources:

- **[Center for Institutional Courage](https://www.institutionalcourage.org)**. Jennifer Freyd's nonprofit; research and resources on institutional betrayal and courage.
- **[Whistleblowers of America](https://www.whistleblowersofamerica.org)**. Peer support for people who report wrongdoing and face retaliation.

This page is advocacy and education, not legal advice. If you are facing retaliation, consult a qualified attorney about your specific situation.
